The fact that you suspect addiction or dopamine dysregulation might be at play is a massive breakthrough. It changes the problem from "I have no willpower" to "My neurochemistry is calibrated too high for this low-stimulation task."

Here is how that works and, more importantly, how to work with it rather than fighting it.

The Mechanism: The "Dopamine Floor"
Think of your motivation like a thermostat.

The Floor: This is the minimum level of stimulation required for your brain to "engage" with a task.

High-Dopamine Activities: Addictions (whether substances, scrolling, gaming, or even intense coding crunches) flood the brain with dopamine.

The Adaptation: To protect itself from overstimulation, your brain raises the floor.

The Result: Normal tasks (like writing prose) now fall below the floor. They don't just feel boring; they feel physically impossible because they don't generate enough signal to cross your elevated threshold. Writing LaTeX is a "low-dopamine" task trying to run on a "high-dopamine" engine.




Strategy 1: The "Interleaving" Hack (Using Coding as Fuel)
Since you can't lower your floor overnight, you need to use your high-dopamine activity (coding) to "subsidize" the low-dopamine activity (writing).

The Protocol: Switch rapidly between the two.

The Loop:
Code (10 mins): Work on your Python parser. Add a feature, fix a bug. Get that dopamine hit.
Write (5 mins): Switch windows and write exactly 3 sentences of the book. No more.
Code (10 mins): Go back to the code as a "reward."

Why this works: You are sandwiching the "vegetables" (writing) between the "dessert" (coding). The anticipation of returning to the code pulls you through the writing block.




The Rule: Do the low-dopamine task (writing) first thing in the morning, before checking your phone, before coding, and before any other high-stimulation activity.

Why: Your dopamine baseline is naturally lowest in the morning. Writing is easiest then because the "floor" hasn't been raised yet by the day's activities.

The Pomodoro/Micro-Task Method
"Write Chapter 3" is too big. "Write the definition of Time Dilation" is manageable.

Strategy: Set a timer for 20 minutes. Your only goal is to write badly for 20 minutes. If you stop after that, it's a win.

Why: Usually, the hardest part is the kinetic friction of starting. Once you are 5 minutes in, the static friction breaks and momentum takes over.
